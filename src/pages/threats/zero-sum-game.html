The Zero-Sum Game

28 February 2026

The Conservative Mindset

Conservatives often view society and wealth through a zero-sum lens, believing that when one group gains, another must lose.

From this perspective, progress is seen not as a rising tide lifting all boats, but as a threat to the status and resources of others.

This mindset shapes much of their political worldview and explains resistance to movements like Civil Rights and Black Lives Matter.

This explains why conservatives often see social and economic progress as a threat to their own status or resources.

For example, many conservatives interpret Black Lives Matter not as a call for justice and equality, but as an attempt to diminish white privilege and “knock white people down a peg”. This reflects the zero-sum assumption that gains for one group necessarily come at the expense of another.

This misinterpretation fuels resistance and backlash. It frames social justice as a battle for scarce resources or status, rather than a shared effort to build a fairer society.

This perspective can be traced to deeper psychological and cultural factors. Fear of loss, uncertainty about the future, and a desire to maintain traditional social hierarchies all contribute to zero-sum thinking. When people feel their identity or livelihood is at risk, they are more likely to adopt defensive, exclusionary attitudes.

Implications

Yes, this worldview is a fallacy. Economic and social progress is not a fixed pie; it can grow and expand. A rising tide does lift all boats, creating opportunities for everyone rather than forcing competition over limited resources.

But, we cannot simply dismiss the zero-sum mindset as irrational. It is a deeply ingrained way of thinking that shapes how many conservatives perceive the world. It influences their fears, motivations, and policy preferences.

We cannot expect to change their entire worldview. Liberals must recognize that conservatives will never abandon their zero-sum worldview. Conservatives will never see that the pie can grow, or that progress benefits everyone. Attempts to convince them that their entire perspective is flawed only backfires, pushing them further toward defensive, far-right positions. Conservatives see any attempt to “educate” them as a perceived threat to their identity, values, and way of life.

The strategic solution is to work with their narrow worldview to find common ground and build support for policy outcomes. Instead of trying to change their minds wholesale, we must shift the terms of debate to focus on shared goals and outcomes in a manner that resonates with their competitive instincts.

Applying the Zero-Sum Game to Mutual Goals

By using the zero-sum framework conservatives hold, we can redirect their competitive instincts toward shared national goals.

One effective approach is to pivot from internal cultural and social battles to external, global competition—particularly with China. We can convince conservatives, who see resources as finite, to support policies that strengthen America’s global leadership, economic power, and security by raming investments in innovation, infrastructure, and international alliances as essential to winning the global zero-sum game. This further appeals to conservative desires for national strength, economic dominance, and protecting American interests.

This strategy does not require conservatives to abandon their worldview but leverages it to build bipartisan support for policies that ultimately benefit all Americans. It transforms zero-sum thinking from a source of division into a tool for unity around mutual goals.

Conclusion

Liberals and the Democratic Party cannot realistically expect to change deeply-held conservative worldviews rooted in zero-sum thinking. Instead, by understanding and working within this mindset, we can craft arguments and policies that align with conservative motivations while advancing broader societal progress. This pragmatic approach offers the best chance to bridge divides and move the country forward together.
